# How to Regenerate a Shot

**Goal:** Adjust a shot that didn't come out right and re-render it without touching anything else in the project.

Shot regeneration is one of the most common actions in ACT3 AI. The AI rarely nails a complex shot on the first try. Iteration is fast — adjust one thing, regenerate, review. Repeat until it's right.

---

## Steps

1. **Play the shot and identify what's wrong.** Watch the rendered clip in the video preview. Is the angle wrong? Is the character's performance flat? Is the lighting too dark? Be specific about what needs to change.

2. **Open the shot panel.** Click the shot in the timeline or shot list.

3. **Adjust the parameter that needs to change.** Common changes:
   - Wrong framing → change the shot type in the Camera tab
   - Wrong action → rewrite the action description
   - Wrong lighting → change the mood in the Lighting tab
   - Camera move isn't working → adjust the Motion tab settings
   - Character performance needs direction → add or revise performance notes in the shot description

4. **Edit the AI prompt directly (advanced).** Click the **Prompt** tab to see the full text that drives the render. You can edit specific phrases — add emphasis, remove conflicting directions, or inject new descriptive language.

5. **Click AI Run.** The shot queues for regeneration. The previous render stays in the timeline until the new one finishes.

6. **Compare old and new.** The shot panel shows the previous render alongside the new one. Pick the better version or keep iterating.

7. **Choose quality level.** Use **Draft** for quick iteration — fast, lower quality, cheap. When you're close to what you want, switch to **Standard** or **High** for the final render.

---

## Tips

- Change one thing at a time. If you adjust too many parameters at once, you won't know which change fixed the problem.
- Use Draft renders for all iteration. Save credits by only doing high-quality renders when you're satisfied with the direction.
- The Prompt tab is your most powerful lever. Small word changes in the action description can dramatically change the output.
