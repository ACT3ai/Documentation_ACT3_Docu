# How to Make a Short Film With AI, From Idea to Final Cut

Making a short film with AI takes seven steps, and only one of them is generating video:

1. **Lock the idea** — a logline, a want, an obstacle, and an ending you can shoot.
2. **Get a script** — write it, or expand the premise into a screenplay with acts, beats, scenes and dialogue.
3. **Define characters and sets once** — appearance, wardrobe, locations. This is what keeps 80 shots looking like one film.
4. **Break the script into a shot list** — beats → scenes → shots, each with camera, lens, movement, lighting and who is present.
5. **Generate first frames** — one reference still per shot, so the look is decided before motion costs you anything.
6. **Generate the video and audio** — clips from the frames and prompts, dialogue from TTS, mouths driven by lipsync.
7. **Assemble and review the whole cut** — not clip by clip, but end to end, then rebuild what does not work.

A five-to-ten-minute short is roughly **80 to 150 shots**. That number is the whole story of why first AI film projects get abandoned: people budget for the fun part (step 6) and drown in steps 3, 4 and 5. This guide is mostly about surviving those.

---

## Step 1: Pick an idea AI can actually shoot

Constraints beat ambition on a first project.

**Favourable:** few characters, few locations, action that is visible, moments that read in short shots, a clear beginning and end.

**Punishing:** crowds, complex prop handling, long continuous takes, fine hand detail, anything whose meaning lives in dialogue subtext with no visual signal.

Write a one-sentence logline. If you cannot see the last shot of the film in your head, keep working on the idea, not the tools.

## Step 2: Get a script

Two routes.

**Write it.** Standard screenplay format. Keep each action paragraph to one visual idea — one paragraph becomes one shot. Use identical location names each time. Spell character names identically.

**Expand it.** If you have a premise rather than a script, AI story expansion turns a logline, an article, a book excerpt or pasted text into a structured screenplay: plot and theme, acts, beats, characters with arcs, then scenes and dialogue. You accept, edit or reject at every level.

Either way, target duration matters. Set the runtime up front so the story is shaped to fit — a short that wants to be four minutes should not be broken down as if it were twelve.

## Step 3: Define characters and sets once

This is the step beginners skip, and it is the reason their films look like slideshows of unrelated images.

**Characters.** For each: name, age, physical appearance, personality, and wardrobe per scene ("Working in Office", "Evening Party"). Do this once, centrally. Never re-describe a character inside a shot prompt.

**Sets.** For each location: what kind of set it is (2D image, 3D model, procedural environment), its key features, and its lighting. Link scenes explicitly to the set so the room stays the same room.

Doing this properly is what makes the difference between 100 shots that cohere and 100 shots that argue with each other.

## Step 4: Break the script into a shot list

Every shot needs more than a description. It needs: shot type, camera movement, lens and height, characters present, primary focus, wardrobe variant, set, lighting, and duration.

You can build this in a spreadsheet by hand. For 120 shots, expect days. Or you can have it computed from the script — which is the entire argument for using a production platform rather than a prompt box.

## Step 5: Generate first frames

Generate one still per shot before generating any motion. Two reasons:

- **Cheaper iteration.** Fixing the look on a still is faster than re-rolling video.
- **Better video.** A first frame anchors the shot's composition, character look and lighting, so the video model has less room to invent.

Review the frames as a sequence. Eyelines, wardrobe, room continuity — catch it here.

## Step 6: Generate video and audio

Now the clips. Alongside them:

- **Dialogue** generated by text-to-speech from the script, per shot, with the spoken length driving how long the shot runs.
- **Lipsync** driven from the audio.
- **Motion capture** if you want a real performance — markerless full-body extraction from ordinary phone video, no suit required.

Do not generate all 120 shots at once on your first pass. Do a scene, watch it, fix what is systematically wrong, then run the rest.

## Step 7: Assemble and review the whole thing

Approved shots stitch into scenes with transitions and audio. Then watch the film end to end, on one timeline, at full length. Clip-by-clip review will not find the problems that matter — pacing that sags in the middle, a costume that changed, two consecutive shots on the same angle.

Then rebuild. The best short films made this way are not first passes; they are the fifth pass, each one incorporating yesterday's notes.

## Realistic expectations for a first short

| Aspect | What to expect |
| --- | --- |
| Shot count | ~80–150 for 5–10 minutes |
| Biggest time sink without automation | Shot list and prompt writing, not rendering |
| Most common failure | Character and set drift between shots |
| Second most common | Pacing — discovered only when watching the full cut |
| Where quality actually comes from | Iteration passes, not the first generation |
| What AI will not do | Make the story good |
