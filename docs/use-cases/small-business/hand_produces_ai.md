# Hire a Team to Make a Video From Your Script

If you have a script and no one in the building who can shoot it, you have three ways to get a video made: hire a production company, hire freelancers and coordinate them yourself, or hire a team that produces the video with AI. The third option is newer and it changes the economics — no location, no crew day, no equipment rental, and revisions that take hours instead of another shoot day. What stays the same is that you hand over a script and get back a finished video.

The ACT 3 **"Level 2 team"** is that third option. It is an optional package where our team takes your script and your feedback and makes the video happen — for part of your production or all of it. You give the notes you want; they get accomplished. You do not learn software, manage vendors, or book anything.

This page is about hiring people to produce a video from a script you already have. It is not a guide to writing the script, and it is not a DIY tutorial.

## What you actually have to supply

Less than you think, and more specifically than you expect:

- **The script or the copy.** A formal script is ideal. Rough copy, a product one-pager, a blog post or a paragraph of intent also work — the platform expands high-level ideas into a full structured script that you approve first.
- **Length and format.** A 30-second spot, a two-minute explainer and a ten-minute training video are different builds. Target duration drives how the story, scenes and shots get paced.
- **Where it will run.** Exports come in 16:9 for YouTube, vertical 9:16 for TikTok and Reels, and 1:1 for Instagram — say which ones you need up front and get them all from one build.
- **Brand rules.** Logo, colour palette and typography compliance are handled with brand-lock controls, so the video looks like your company and not like generic stock.
- **Who approves.** One decision-maker, ideally. Two is workable. Six is where projects die.

## How the engagement runs

1. **Kickoff.** You hand over the script, the runtime, the platforms and the brand assets. We agree the visual style — Cinematic Realism, 3D Animated, Cartoon 2D or Anime — and what the video must accomplish.
2. **We produce.** The script is imported and auto-formatted, broken into scenes and shots with camera, lens and lighting decisions attached, and produced through an automated pipeline: prompts assembled per shot, first frames generated, characters built with the right wardrobe, sets sourced, dialogue voiced with built-in text-to-speech and lip-synced, shots rendered and assembled with transitions and audio.
3. **You review a finished video.** Not a storyboard. A watchable cut.
4. **You give notes.** Any level: "make the second half faster," "the presenter's jacket is wrong," "swap the office for a warehouse," "reshoot the closing line." Time-stamped feedback keeps rounds unambiguous.
5. **We revise.** Revisions regenerate rather than re-shoot, so a change that would have meant another crew day is another pass.
6. **You get the files.** MP4/MOV, multi-format exports for each platform, and professional masters if you need them for broadcast or an agency partner.

The strategic argument is straightforward: virtual sets remove location and permit costs, generation removes crew days, and the pre-production pipeline that traditionally consumes 80–200 hours compresses to roughly two. That is why speed to publish moves from weeks to days.

## When to hire a team instead of doing it in-house

Hire a team if:

- Nobody in-house has produced video before, and you do not want the first attempt to be the campaign that matters.
- You need it in days, not next quarter.
- Your script is done and the bottleneck is purely execution.
- You have a burst of work — a product launch, a series of training modules — and no permanent video headcount.

Do it in-house on ACT 3 AI if:

- You will make video continuously and want the capability internally.
- You want hands on every shot.
- You have someone who enjoys the tooling.

A common and sensible hybrid: we produce the first videos while your team learns the platform, then you take it over. The production lives in a workspace your organization owns either way, so the handover is a permission change, not a migration.

## Ownership, rights and the things procurement will ask

- **Ownership.** The Organization — your workspace — legally owns the projects, created content and generated assets, as stated in the Terms of Service. Ownership can be transferred to another member with confirmation from both parties.
- **Commercial use.** Commercial-use rights are a plan-level feature; business tiers include them. Make sure the plan your production runs on matches your intended use.
- **Content safety.** Three-stage moderation scans prompts before generation, scripts before production and finished outputs before download, with redaction or admin review options.
- **Access control.** Isolated multi-tenant workspaces, optional SAML SSO, invitation-only project access, and per-role permissions from Read up to Owner. Reviewers can watch without being able to change anything.
- **Retention.** Storage quotas scale by plan and lower tiers remove dormant assets after four months — worth checking if you plan to archive rather than export.

## What we will tell you honestly

- If your script needs work, we will say so before producing it. A weak script produces a weak video faster.
- Photoreal human presenters delivering long pieces to camera are the hardest thing to do well. Often the better answer is a different treatment, not a bigger budget.
- If you need documentary footage of your actual factory, that is a camera job. Use AI for everything around it.
- Very heavy brand-compliance regimes need their rules stated up front, not discovered in round three.
