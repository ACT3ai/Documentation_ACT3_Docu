# How to Make a Product Launch Video With AI Before Your Deadline

**You can produce a launch video with AI by writing or importing the script, letting the platform automate the shot list, first frames, prompts, voiceover, and assembly, then reviewing and regenerating the shots that need work — typically in days rather than the weeks an agency or a shoot requires.** The reason it fits a launch deadline is not that AI generates a clip fast. It is that the *unglamorous* work — building the shot list, writing a prompt per shot, generating a starting frame per shot, keeping the product and presenter looking the same throughout, cutting it together — gets automated instead of done by a person at 11pm.

If your launch is in two weeks and you do not have a video, this page is the practical path: what a launch video needs to contain, the realistic timeline, where AI genuinely saves you and where it does not, and how to avoid the mistakes that produce something you are embarrassed to post.

## What a Launch Video Actually Needs

Before tooling, get the shape right. Most small-business launch videos fail on structure, not production value.

| Section | Duration | Job |
|---|---|---|
| Hook | 0:00–0:05 | Name the problem in one line, visually |
| Problem | 0:05–0:20 | Make the pain concrete and specific |
| Reveal | 0:20–0:35 | Show the product doing the thing |
| Proof | 0:35–1:00 | Demonstrate, don't assert |
| Who it's for | 1:00–1:15 | Let the right buyer self-identify |
| CTA | 1:15–1:30 | One action, stated plainly |

Ninety seconds is the sweet spot for a launch hero video. You will also want cutdowns — a 30-second version for paid, a 15-second version and a vertical cut for social — which is a production multiplier worth planning for on day one rather than scrambling for later.

## The Realistic Timeline

Here is a workable schedule for a launch two weeks out.

**Days 1–2 — Script and structure.** Write the 90-second script against the shape above, or paste rough copy and let AI story expansion structure it. Set the target duration up front so the scenes and shots are shaped to fit 90 seconds rather than trimmed down from four minutes.

**Day 3 — Look and cast.** Choose a style preset (cinematic realism, 3D animated, cartoon 2D, or anime) and commit to it. Define any on-screen characters once, with wardrobe. Define your locations as sets. This is the day that determines whether the video looks coherent.

**Days 4–6 — Generate the first full pass.** Automated shot list from the beats and scenes, automatic first frames, automatically composed video prompts and first-frame prompts, TTS voiceover driving lipsync timing, multi-model render, and automatic scene assembly with transitions and audio into a production-ready cut.

**Day 7 — Watch it whole, take notes.** Play the assembled 90 seconds start to finish. Note pacing and clarity problems, not individual frames.

**Days 8–10 — Regenerate and refine.** One-click regeneration per shot for lighting, pacing, or mood. Fix the three shots that are hurting the video rather than tuning all thirty.

**Days 11–12 — Cutdowns and exports.** One-click export for 16:9, vertical 9:16, and 1:1 from the same production. Build the 30s and 15s cuts from the same assets.

**Days 13–14 — Buffer.** Something will need another pass. Leave the room.

## What AI Won't Do for You

Be clear-eyed, because launch videos are public.

- **It won't write your positioning.** If the script does not name a real problem in a real customer's words, no amount of production quality rescues it.
- **It won't produce a photoreal screen recording of your actual software.** For a SaaS product, record the real UI and cut it in. AI handles the narrative wrapper — the customer, the problem, the world — not your product's literal interface.
- **It won't guarantee photoreal human faces at hero-shot scale.** Stylized approaches hold up better. If the video hinges on a photoreal close-up of a person's face, plan accordingly.
- **It won't hide inconsistency.** Locking style, cast, and sets before generating is not optional, and skipping it is the number one cause of a launch video that looks cheap.
- **It isn't free.** Generation is metered by credits. Cost is shown before every job and the render queue displays predicted spend, so it is predictable — but budget for it.

## Practical Tips That Make the Difference

1. **Commit to one style preset.** Mixed styles are the single most obvious tell of a rushed AI video.
2. **Write to 90 seconds, not to your enthusiasm.** Set the target duration and let the pacing be computed against it.
3. **Generate a full pass before perfecting any shot.** You will discard shots. Do not polish them first.
4. **Watch it as a whole every pass.** Approving shots individually produces videos that are fine shot-by-shot and incoherent overall.
5. **Plan the cutdowns on day one.** Exporting 9:16 and 1:1 from the same production is trivial if you planned for it and painful if you did not.
6. **Get the moderation pass in early.** Prompts, scripts, and outputs are scanned before generation, before production, and before download — useful to know for anything brand-sensitive.
