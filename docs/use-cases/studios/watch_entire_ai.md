# How to Review a Two-Hour Movie on One Timeline

Reviewing a two-hour movie on a single timeline means loading the entire cut — every shot, in order, with audio — into one continuous sequence you can play end to end, scrub freely, and zoom from the full-feature overview down to a single frame. It is how editorial has always judged a film: not shot by shot, but as one uninterrupted runtime where pacing, rhythm, and continuity are visible.

For AI-generated films this is the step that most often does not exist. Clip-based workflows leave you with a folder of generations and no honest way to answer "does the movie work?" You can love every shot and still have a film that drags in act two, repeats the same coverage six times, or breaks continuity in a way nobody notices until a viewer does.

## What only a full-runtime review can tell you

Some problems are invisible at any smaller scale:

- **Act-level pacing.** Whether the second act sags is a 40-minute observation, not a 4-second one.
- **Shot rhythm and repetition.** Six medium two-shots in a row read as monotony only in sequence.
- **Transition quality.** How a shot lands into the next depends entirely on what precedes it.
- **Continuity across distance.** A wardrobe change between scene 12 and scene 84 is only caught when both play in one pass.
- **Runtime truth.** Whether you actually have 98 minutes or 112, and where the fat is.
- **Emotional build.** Whether the ending earns what came before.
- **Audio flow.** Whether dialogue levels, TTS delivery, and silences hold up across the film.

None of these are shot notes. They are film notes, and film notes require the film.

## Why AI workflows usually skip it

Four practical reasons, all fixable:

1. **Output is scattered.** Clips land as individual files, named by generation job, not by scene and shot.
2. **Assembly is manual.** Someone has to import, order, and conform 1,200 clips — a job nobody wants to redo after every revision.
3. **Scrubbing is painful.** Large numbers of generated clips in an NLE without proper caching means black frames and stuttering.
4. **Revisions break the sequence.** Regenerating shots invalidates the assembly, so nobody rebuilds it often.

The result is that teams review sequences at best, and often just dailies of individual shots.

## A practical full-runtime review process

1. **Assemble the current cut** — let approved shots stitch automatically; do not hand-conform.
2. **Watch it straight through, once, without stopping.** Take timecode notes; resist the urge to fix.
3. **Second pass by act.** Use selection-based playback per act; judge shape and length.
4. **Third pass for continuity.** Watch character-by-character where possible — wardrobe, look, voice.
5. **Sort notes by level** — story/beat, scene, shot, character, set, style. Level-tagged notes are the ones that survive a rebuild.
6. **Lock what works.** Freeze approved pages, scenes, and shots as read-only before the next generation pass.
7. **Rebuild and screen again.** ACT 3 can rebuild the entire movie from the prior day's feedback, so tomorrow you screen a new complete cut. See our guide to rebuilding your movie daily from notes.

## Honest notes

- **Full-runtime review is a discipline, not just a feature.** The timeline makes it possible; someone still has to sit and watch two hours.
- **Do it early and often.** The first full screening should happen on a rough cut, not near delivery.
- **Screen with the decision-makers.** A shared screening produces better, more consistent notes than distributed clip review.
- **Expect the first screening to hurt.** That is the point — better a painful screening on day 5 than a painful one at delivery.
