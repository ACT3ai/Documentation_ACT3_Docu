# AI Video Generator for Startups Shipping Content Weekly

**Short answer:** a startup marketing team does not have a video quality problem. It has a **cadence** problem. One person owns content, video is the highest-effort format, and so video becomes the thing that ships once a quarter instead of once a week. An AI video generator fixes this only if it automates the whole pipeline — script structure, shot planning, prompt writing, first frames, narration, and assembly — not just the render. If you still hand-write a prompt per shot, you have replaced a video editor with a prompt engineer and your cadence has not moved.

The setup that actually produces weekly output: paste a rough idea or a blog post, get a structured script and shot list back, review and fix the story, render a draft of the whole thing, finish the parts that work, and export every aspect ratio at once. On ACT 3 AI that pipeline is automated end to end, on plans starting at $0 free, $8/month Community, $35/month Standard, and $175/month Business, which is where commercial-use rights begin.

## Why weekly video breaks small teams

Do the arithmetic on the traditional loop and it is obvious why it fails:

| Step | Typical time for a 60-second piece | Automatable? |
|---|---|---|
| Concept and script | 2–4 hours | Mostly |
| Shot list and storyboard | 2–3 hours | Yes |
| Sourcing footage or writing prompts | 3–8 hours | Yes |
| Generation / shooting | 1–4 hours | Yes |
| Voiceover | 1–2 hours | Yes |
| Edit and assembly | 3–6 hours | Largely |
| Aspect-ratio variants | 1–2 hours | Yes |

That is one to two full days per video for one person who also runs the newsletter, the launch, and the paid account. Weekly is not a discipline problem. It is a math problem.

Automation changes the math by collapsing the middle of the table — the mechanical steps between "we know what to say" and "there is a file." Those steps carry no creative value and consume most of the time.

## A weekly cadence that a one-person team can hold

**Batch day, twice a month (about 3 hours):**

1. Write four short briefs — one per planned video. Just the argument, not the script.
2. Paste each in, set duration, and let the pipeline build script, shots, and prompts.
3. Review all four shot lists back to back. Fix the story, not the pixels. This is the only step where your judgment is irreplaceable.
4. Draft-render all four at the cheapest quality tier and watch them.
5. Kill what does not work. Re-block and re-render what nearly works.
6. Finish the survivors at high quality, add narration, export three aspect ratios each.

**Weekly (about 20 minutes):** schedule, caption, post, and log what performed.

Two batch days a month produces a weekly cadence with room for one reactive video when something happens. That is the whole game.

## Keeping quality up while volume goes up

- **Hold one style preset.** Cinematic Realism, 3D Animated, Cartoon 2D, or Anime — pick one and keep it. Consistency reads as a brand.
- **Reuse a character.** A recurring on-screen presence, locked by per-character consistency, compounds recognition across a quarter.
- **Upload style images** as a visual reference so generated looks track your brand rather than a generic default.
- **Write for the first two seconds.** No production value rescues a slow open.
- **Keep a real clip in the mix.** Founder to camera, product screen, customer moment — authenticity raises the trust of everything around it.
