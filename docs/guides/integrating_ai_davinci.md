# How to Integrate AI Video With Premiere (and DaVinci) Post Workflows

**Short answer:** you integrate AI-generated video into Adobe Premiere Pro or DaVinci Resolve the same way you integrate any other source — by delivering it as conformable media plus an edit decision list, not as a folder of loose clips. Concretely: render each shot to a mastering codec (ProRes is the safe default), keep a strict shot-naming convention that matches your shot list, export an **EDL** so the assembly rebuilds itself on the NLE timeline, and bring audio in as separate stems so dialogue and effects stay editable. Do that and AI shots behave like dailies from any other unit.

The three things that break AI-to-Premiere workflows are always the same:

* **No conform data.** Clips arrive with generated filenames and no ordering, so an editor rebuilds the cut by hand.
* **Web-delivery codecs.** Highly compressed MP4 does not hold up through color and effects.
* **Re-generation churn.** A shot gets regenerated upstream and nobody knows which timeline clip to replace.

This guide walks the full round trip: what to export, how to conform it, how to keep versions straight, and how to review a whole feature rather than a pile of clips.

## The handoff format: what to actually export

Decide these four things before the first render.

| Deliverable | Use it for | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **ProRes masters** | The picture itself | Mastering-grade codec that survives grading and VFX; the standard studio hand-off |
| **MP4 / MOV** | Review copies, client links, social cuts | Small, universally playable, not for finishing |
| **EDL** | Rebuilding the assembly on the NLE timeline | Carries the cut order so the editor does not conform by hand |
| **FDX / PDF script** | Keeping script and cut in sync | Editors and script supervisors work from the screenplay, not a spreadsheet |

## Step 1: Lock a naming convention before you generate anything

The single highest-leverage habit. Name every shot so the filename tells the editor where it belongs:

```
SHOW_S01E03_SC012_SH004_v03.mov
```

Show, season/episode, scene, shot, version. When a shot is regenerated, only the version increments — the slot in the timeline is unambiguous. Any structured platform gives you this for free because the project is already hierarchical (show → season → episode → scene → shot); the failure mode is exporting from a tool that has no structure and inventing names afterward.

## Step 2: Conform in Premiere or Resolve via EDL, not by dragging

Import the EDL, point it at the media folder, and let the NLE build the sequence. Then:

1. **Verify frame rate and timebase match.** Mismatched frame rates are the most common cause of drifting sync in AI-sourced cuts.
2. **Check handles.** Generated clips are often cut tight; ask for a little extra at head and tail where the shot allows it.
3. **Relink to ProRes, review on MP4.** Cut against proxies if you like, but finish against masters.
4. **Bring audio as separate tracks.** Dialogue generated by text-to-speech, effects, and music should not be baked into the picture render — you want them independently editable.

DaVinci Resolve follows the same path: EDL conform, ProRes media, separate audio. Resolve's advantage is the grade; the reason to keep AI shots in a mastering codec is precisely so the grade has room to work.

## Step 3: Plan for regeneration — the part traditional post does not prepare you for

Traditional post assumes picture is captured once. AI production assumes any shot can be regenerated in minutes. That inverts the version problem: instead of "we can never get that shot again," it becomes "we have eleven versions of that shot and the editor has v3."

Three rules keep it sane:

* **Version in the filename, never overwrite.** v01, v02, v03 — an editor should never wonder whether a file changed under them.
* **Regenerate the shot, not the sequence.** Replace one clip in the timeline; the EDL slot stays valid.
* **Keep a change log per scene.** "SC012_SH004 regenerated for lighting" saves an hour of comparison.

## Step 4: Review the whole runtime, not a reel of clips

Here is where most AI video workflows quietly fail, and where the integration question really matters for studio teams.

Reviewing AI shots in a grid of thumbnails tells you whether each clip is good. It tells you nothing about whether the *film* is good. Pacing, continuity, wardrobe drift, performance energy across a scene, whether an act lands — none of that is visible one clip at a time. It is only visible on a unified timeline at full length.

## FAQ

**What is the best export format for AI video going into Premiere?**
ProRes for anything that will be graded or finished; MP4 or MOV for review and delivery. Keep audio as separate tracks rather than baked into the picture render.

**Do I need an EDL if I only have a few shots?**
No — for a short piece, dragging clips in is fine. The EDL earns its keep the moment a project has enough shots that manual conform becomes error-prone, which is most narrative work.

**Does this work with DaVinci Resolve as well as Premiere?**
Yes. The pattern is identical — conform from an EDL, work against ProRes, keep audio separate. ACT 3 AI is designed for compatibility with both Premiere Pro and DaVinci Resolve.

**How do I handle a shot that gets regenerated after picture is in the NLE?**
Export the new version with an incremented version suffix and swap that single clip. Never overwrite the file the editor is already cutting with.

**Can I see the whole movie before it leaves the platform?**
Yes, and you should. Reviewing the full 1-to-2-hour runtime on a unified timeline — in-platform or on the Premiere timeline — is the only reliable way to catch pacing and continuity problems that per-clip review misses.

**What about frame rate?**
Set it once at the project level and never change it mid-production. Mismatched timebases between generated media and the NLE sequence are the most common conform failure.
