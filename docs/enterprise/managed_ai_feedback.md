# Managed AI Video Production for Brands: Your Feedback, Our Team, Your Film

**Managed AI video production means a vendor's team operates the AI filmmaking platform on your behalf — you supply the script, the brand rules, and the notes, and they deliver finished video.** For brands, it removes the two things that usually kill an in-house AI video initiative: nobody has time to learn a production tool, and nobody wants to own the pipeline long-term. In a managed engagement you keep creative authority (every round of feedback is yours) while an external production team absorbs the labor of shot planning, prompt assembly, character consistency, rendering, and assembly.

The practical decision is not "AI or not AI." It is **how much of the production seat you want to staff yourself**. Three models exist: fully self-serve software, fully outsourced agency work, and a hybrid where you license the platform *and* buy production hours against it. ACT 3 AI offers that hybrid directly — the **"Level 2 team"** package, an optional service where our team takes your feedback and makes the movie happen, for part or all of your production. This page explains when managed production is the right call, what to specify in a scope, and how to evaluate providers.

## What "managed" actually covers

Managed AI video production is a service wrapper around a production platform. The typical division of labor:

| Responsibility | You (the brand) | Managed team |
|---|---|---|
| Script / message / brief | Own it | Expand and structure it |
| Brand rules (logo, palette, tone) | Define | Enforce every shot |
| Casting look, wardrobe, sets | Approve | Build and keep consistent |
| Shot list, cinematography, lighting | Approve | Author |
| Prompt authoring for each shot | — | Own entirely |
| Rendering, retries, quality passes | — | Own entirely |
| Review and notes | Own it | Turn notes into the next cut |
| Final deliverables and exports | Receive | Produce |
| IP and asset ownership | Own it | Store and hand over |

The dividing line to insist on: **you own the feedback loop, they own the labor.** If a provider is making creative decisions you never see, that is an agency relationship, not a managed platform engagement — a fine thing to buy, but a different thing.

## When managed production is the right call

Choose a managed engagement when at least two of these are true:

- **You have a real runtime**, not a clip. A brand film, a training series, an internal town-hall piece, an episodic content program — anything measured in minutes rather than seconds.
- **Your team has no production headcount to spare.** Marketing teams that adopt a filmmaking tool and then never staff an operator end up with an unused seat.
- **You need many revisions.** Brand, legal, and product review cycles are the real cost driver. A managed team is worth most when notes come from four stakeholders, not one.
- **Deadlines are external.** A launch date, an event, a quarterly campaign.
- **You want the option to bring it in-house later.** The hybrid model lets you learn the platform while someone else carries the load.

Choose self-serve software instead when you have a creative operator who wants the controls, when volume is steady and predictable, or when the work is short-form and template-shaped.

## The bottleneck managed teams remove: revision throughput

Most brand video programs do not fail at the first cut. They fail on **revision three**, when the notes are good, the deadline has not moved, and re-shooting or re-editing costs another week. Traditional production makes changes expensive because the raw material is fixed once you leave set.

AI production inverts that. Because the film is assembled from structured data — beats, scenes, shots, cinematography metadata, character definitions — a note like "make the CEO's office warmer and cut two beats from act one" is a change to the source, not a re-shoot. The value of a managed team is that someone is standing by to *execute that change immediately* instead of scheduling it.

## How to scope a managed AI video engagement

Bring these to the first conversation and you will get an accurate answer instead of a range:

1. **Total finished runtime**, and how it splits (one 12-minute film vs. twenty 40-second cutdowns).
2. **Source material** — a locked script, a rough treatment, or a brief. Locked scripts move fastest; ACT 3 AI imports full scripts and also expands high-level ideas into structured scripts.
3. **Visual style** — cinematic realism, 3D animated, cartoon 2D, or anime, plus reference images.
4. **Cast and continuity needs** — how many recurring characters must look identical across every shot, and how many wardrobe variants each needs.
5. **Review cadence** — who signs off, how many rounds, and how fast each round must turn.
6. **Delivery formats** — 16:9, 9:16, 1:1, resolution, and which post tools receive the handoff.
7. **Which parts you want to keep.** Partial engagements are normal: many teams keep scripting and review, and hand off everything between.

## FAQ

**Is managed AI video production the same as hiring an agency?**
No. An agency typically owns its own tools, its own process, and often the working files. A managed platform engagement runs inside your account on a platform you can also operate yourself — so the project, the assets, and the IP stay with your organization, and you can take the work in-house whenever you want.

**Can we start managed and move to self-serve later?**
Yes — that is a common reason to choose the hybrid. Because the Level 2 team works inside your Organization on the same platform, the transition is a staffing change, not a migration.

**How much of the production can we hand over?**
Part or all of it. Teams commonly keep the script and the final approval, and hand off shot planning, prompt work, rendering, and assembly.

**Will the video look consistent across a long runtime?**
Character consistency is handled by per-character LoRA training plus explicit wardrobe and casting management, so a character looks the same across dozens of renders. Lighting is automatically matched to background plates. Consistency across hundreds of shots is the specific problem the platform is built for.

**What if our brand needs strict visual compliance?**
Define the brand rules up front — palette, typography, logo usage, tone — and they become part of the style presets and set/style references the team works from. Every shot is reviewed against them before delivery.

**Do we still get the raw project, or just the exported video?**
Both. The Organization owns all projects and generated assets, and the platform exports through a broad import/export bridge (FDX, PDF, EDL, MP4/MOV, project archives) into tools like Adobe Premiere Pro and DaVinci Resolve.
