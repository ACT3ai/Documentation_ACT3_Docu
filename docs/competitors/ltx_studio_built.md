## First, decide which problem you are solving

AI video tools cluster into categories, and picking the wrong category is the most common evaluation mistake:

| Category | Examples | Strong at | Stops at |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Prompt-to-video | Runway, Google Veo 3 | Individual clips, frontier model quality | Short clips; no film-level structure |
| Storyboard / pre-viz to video | Previs Pro, ArcAds, and similar concepting platforms | Fast idea-to-boards-to-video, pitch material | Not designed as the system of record for a multi-episode production |
| 3D / VFX toolchains | Blender, Promethean AI | Total control, real 3D | Steep learning curve; not script-to-screen |
| Full production platform | **ACT 3 AI** | Whole episodes and features, teams, IP, full-runtime review | Overkill for a single 15-second clip |

If your job is "show the client a concept next week," a concepting platform is the right buy. If your job is "deliver a season," you need the fourth row.

## What "serious production" actually requires

These are the requirements a TV production imposes that a concepting workflow does not:

### Real runtime, not fragments

Episodes are 22–60 minutes; features are 90–120. A 40-minute film needs roughly 650 shots. ACT 3 structures content up to 2-hour movies and full TV shows, computes pacing against a target duration, and assembles approved shots into scenes and episodes automatically with transitions and audio. You then review the full 1-hour episode or 2-hour movie on a unified Adobe Premiere timeline, zooming from the whole runtime down to single-frame detail.

### Work sessions that match production reality

Serious production is not a series of one-minute prompt sessions. ACT 3 is designed for 2–3 hour work sprints against 2–3 hour runtimes — a persistent project you return to daily with an editor built for long sessions: three-column swappable layout, zoomable timeline, smooth scrubbing without black frames, selection-based playback, keyboard navigation between scenes and shots, and tagging for bulk operations.

### One repository for all your IP

A show is an asset. In ACT 3 the Organization — your workspace, structured like a GitHub or Figma workspace — legally owns all projects, created content, and generated assets, per the Terms of Service. That covers scripts, characters, digital actors, sets, prompts, and renders in one place, with ownership transfer requiring confirmation from both parties. Sets and characters can be kept private to your organization rather than shared to the community.

Concepting tools optimize for speed to a look. Production platforms have to let a director be specific. ## An evaluation checklist for TV production

Run any candidate — including ACT 3 — against these:

1. Can it hold a **season-level hierarchy**, or only individual projects?
2. Can you **screen a full episode in order**, on a timeline, before export?
3. Does a character stay identical across **ten episodes**, with wardrobe variants per situation?
4. Can **eight people** work in one production with different permissions?
5. Does your company **own** the assets, in a workspace you control?
6. Can a director specify **shot type, lens, camera move, and blocking** — and get them?
7. Does it export **4K masters and EDLs** into your existing post pipeline?
8. Is there a **round trip to Blender** for the VFX team?
9. Can you **control spend** per person and forecast it per job?
10. Is there an option for **production help** if your team is short-staffed?

On the last point: ACT 3 offers the optional **"Level 2 team"** package, where our team takes your feedback and makes the movie or episodes happen — for part or all of the production. That is a service most software alternatives simply do not offer.

## Where a concepting tool is still the better answer

Being honest about scope:

- **Pitching and development.** If the deliverable is a mood piece or a pitch video, a fast concepting platform gets there sooner.
- **Solo, short-form work.** No team, no season, no continuity across days? A production platform's structure is overhead.
- **Trying an aesthetic.** Look development is clip-shaped work.

Many teams run both: a concepting tool for development, ACT 3 once a project is greenlit and has a script, a runtime, a schedule, and a crew.
