# AI Production Service vs Software-Only: When to Let a Team Drive

**Short answer:** buy **software-only** when you have people who will operate it, a slate that justifies building the skill, and enough runway to absorb a learning curve. Buy an **AI production service** when the deadline is fixed, the team is already committed elsewhere, or you need to prove the approach on a real project before you staff around it. The two are not opposites — the best arrangement for most studios is software you own plus a service team you can dial up and down, on the *same* production.

That is how ACT 3 AI is structured. You get the platform, and the **ACT 3 "Level 2 team"** is an optional package where our team takes your feedback and makes the movie happen — for part or all of your production team. The project stays in your workspace either way, so the decision is reversible.

## The two models, defined precisely

**Software-only.** You license the platform. Your people import the script, build the shot list, cast digital actors, block scenes, run generation batches, review, iterate and deliver. You own the skill and the throughput ceiling is your headcount.

**Production service.** A team operates the pipeline on your behalf. You supply direction and feedback; they supply execution. Your throughput is contracted rather than hired, and your people spend their time on creative decisions instead of platform operations.

The trap is treating this as a permanent identity choice. It is a per-project — often per-phase — resourcing decision.

## Comparison

| Dimension | Software-only | AI production service |
|---|---|---|
| Time to first output | Slower — learning curve first | Faster — the team already knows the pipeline |
| Cost shape | Subscription plus credits, mostly fixed | Service plus platform usage, scales with scope |
| Skill retention | Builds permanently in your team | Builds in the vendor unless you co-work |
| Throughput ceiling | Your headcount | Contracted capacity |
| Control granularity | Total, at every shot | Direction and notes; execution delegated |
| Best for | Ongoing slate, recurring series | Deadlines, spikes, pilots, proofs |
| Risk if you are wrong | Sunk learning time | Dependency, unless the work stays portable |

## Six questions that decide it

**1. Do you have someone who will actually own the pipeline?**
Not "someone interested." Someone with the hours. AI production is real production work — a 40-minute film is roughly 650 shots. If nobody owns it, software-only becomes shelfware.

**2. How fixed is the deadline?**
A hard air date or launch date argues for a service. A development slate with flexible internal milestones argues for building the skill.

**3. Is this one project or a slate?**
A single pilot rarely justifies the learning investment. A season, or a recurring campaign, absolutely does.

**4. How specific is your creative direction?**
Services work brilliantly when direction is clear and the work is execution. If the creative is still being discovered, having your own people in the tool exploring is worth more.

**5. What is the cost of being late versus the cost of staying dependent?**
Late usually costs more in the short term. Dependency costs more over years. Which horizon are you optimizing?

**6. Can you change your mind later?**
This is the one most people skip, and it is the most important. If the project is portable, you can start with a service and bring it in-house — or the reverse — without a migration.

## Why the hybrid usually wins

The strongest pattern we see is not either/or. It is: **own the software, rent the execution where you are short.**

Concretely, a studio might keep the script, the creative direction and final approval fully in-house, and hand the Level 2 team the operating work — shot list construction, casting and wardrobe setup, batch generation, assembly, and applying notes between cuts. As internal people learn the platform on the same project, the boundary moves. Nothing has to be handed back because nothing ever left.

## FAQ

**What is the difference between an AI production service and AI production software?**
Software is the platform your team operates. A service is a team that operates the platform for you. With ACT 3 you can have both on the same project — the Level 2 team is an optional package layered on the software you already own.

**Is a production service more expensive than software?**
It costs more per project because you are buying execution as well as tooling. Whether it is more expensive overall depends on the alternative — hiring, training, and the cost of missing the date.

**Can we start with the service and move in-house later?**
Yes, and that is the main structural advantage. The production lives in your Organization from the start, so moving in-house is a change of who has which permissions, not a migration or a handover.

**Do we lose creative control with a service?**
No — the model is explicitly feedback-driven: you give the notes and they get made. You can also stay inside the workspace throughout, reviewing, commenting and locking approved material as it goes.

**Who owns the finished film either way?**
Your Organization. It legally owns the projects and generated assets under the Terms of Service, regardless of who was operating the platform.

**Can we use the team for only one phase?**
Yes. Part or all — a single sequence, one phase of production, or the whole picture.
